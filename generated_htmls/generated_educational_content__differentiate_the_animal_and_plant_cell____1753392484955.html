### Understanding the Differences: A Journey Inside the Cell
Let's explore the key features that set animal and plant cells apart. Imagine each cell as a tiny, self-sufficient "ghar" (home) or "mohalla" (neighbourhood) with different residents (organelles) performing specific duties.

English:
We'll focus on these main differentiating factors:

1. Cell Wall (рдХреЛрд╢рд┐рдХрд╛ рднрд┐рддреНрддрд┐ / ╪п█М┘И╪з╪▒┘Р ╪о┘Д█М█Б):
Plant Cells: Have a rigid, outer protective layer called the cell wall, made primarily of cellulose. Think of it like the strong, thick walls of an ancient Indian fort (Qila or Garh), like the Red Fort or Mehrangarh Fort. These walls provide structural support, shape, and protection to the plant, helping it stand tall against gravity and wind. It's why plants don't collapse like a deflated balloon!
 Animal Cells: Do not have a cell wall. Their outermost boundary is the flexible cell membrane. This flexibility allows animals to move freely and change shape, much like a dancer performing various mudras (hand gestures) or a child playing Kabaddi, adapting to different movements.

2. Chloroplasts (рд╣рд░рд┐рддрд▓рд╡рдХ / ┌й┘Д┘И╪▒┘И┘╛┘Д╪з╪│┘╣):
Plant Cells: Contain chloroplasts, which are specialized organelles containing chlorophyll. These are the "food factories" or "solar panels" of the plant cell. Just as many Indian households rely on homemade food (and some even have rooftop solar panels!), plants use chloroplasts to perform photosynthesis (рдкреНрд░рдХрд╛рд╢ рд╕рдВрд╢реНрд▓реЗрд╖рдг / ╪╢█М╪з╪ж█М ╪к╪з┘Д█М┘Б). They convert sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose (food). This is why plants are called producers or Annadata (food-givers).
 Animal Cells: Do not have chloroplasts. Animals are consumers; they get their food by eating plants or other animals. Think of how we prepare our delicious biryani or dal-roti тАУ we depend on ingredients from outside, not make them from sunlight!

3. Vacuoles (рд░рд╕рдзрд╛рдиреА / ╪о╪з┘Д█М█Б):
Plant Cells: Typically have a single, large, centrally located vacuole that can occupy 50-90% of the cell volume. This large vacuole is like the main talab (pond) or baoli (stepwell) in an Indian village, storing water, nutrients, waste products, and maintaining turgor pressure (рд╕реНрдлреАрддрд┐ рджрд╛рдм / ╪к┘Ж╪з╪д ╪п╪и╪з╪д) to keep the cell firm.
 Animal Cells: Usually have several small, temporary vacuoles, or sometimes none at all. These are more like small matkas (clay pots) or water bottles carried by individuals for immediate use, rather than a large community reservoir. They primarily store water, ions, and waste for a short period.

4. Centrosomes (рддрд╛рд░рдХрдХрд╛рдп / ╪│┘Ж┘╣╪▒┘И╪│┘И┘Е):
Plant Cells: Higher plants do not have centrosomes. Cell division in plants is managed differently.
 Animal Cells: Present in nearly all animal cells, containing centrioles. Centrosomes play a crucial role in cell division, helping to organize the mitotic spindle. Imagine it as the "traffic police" guiding chromosomes during cell division, ensuring proper distribution.

5. Shape (рдЖрдХрд╛рд░ / ╪┤┌й┘Д):
Plant Cells: Generally have a fixed, regular, and often rectangular or square shape due to the rigid cell wall. Think of a meticulously drawn Rangoli pattern, with its defined edges.
 Animal Cells: Tend to have an irregular or rounded shape due to the absence of a cell wall and the flexibility of the cell membrane. They can change shape easily, like water flowing freely in a river or a fluid Bharatnatyam dancer.

6. Storage Material (рднрдВрдбрд╛рд░рдг рд╕рд╛рдордЧреНрд░реА / ╪░╪о█М╪▒█Б ┘Е┘И╪з╪п):
Plant Cells: Store food primarily as starch (рдордВрдб / ┘Ж╪┤╪з╪│╪к█Б). Just like farmers store gehu (wheat) or chawal (rice) in large granaries.
 Animal Cells: Store food primarily as glycogen (рдЧреНрд▓рд╛рдЗрдХреЛрдЬрди / ┌п┘Д╪з╪ж┌й┘И╪м┘Ж) and fats. Glycogen is like a quick energy reserve, similar to the ladoos or barfis we might keep for an immediate energy boost during a festival.
